# Domains, Boundaries, and Information Friction

![street line drawing of New York City](blog/images/domains-boundaries-and-information-friction.svg)

*By Kin Lane ([API Evangelist](https://apievangelist.com)) · July 18, 2026*

I spend a lot of time thinking about domains and boundaries and how to reduce friction across them. Most people I encounter in my field of work approach this with a positive, utopian view, with how to define everything possible within a domain, with an intent to change behavior within the domain. I tend to approach things by embracing the messy world we have, mapping whatever is already happening within a domain or bounded context, with the intent to understand the best way to share information, expertise, and process across boundaries, with the hope of evolving behavior.

As I sift through the now 85 domains catalogued, and 70 domains assessed across the system that is New York City, I am looking to understand the best ways in which I can share information, tell stories, and reduce friction for citizens in New York City. Sure, I want to better understand how people who work within the city of New York operate within these domains, but finding the stories that matter to citizens of New York City, and identifying the processes that would improve the lives of citizens of New York City across these domains seem more compelling to me right now.

I can see how modular API and MCP design help reduce friction across boundaries. I can see how schema awareness and reuse can reduce friction across boundaries. I can see how Agent Skills can help capture the stories of what cross-domain processes exist, and how they matter to citizens. People who are stewards of specific datasets, and even specific APIs, tend to have their silo blinders on. They live within a silo. They are stewards of something within that silo. They don't spend a lot of time thinking outside of that silo, let alone across domains and boundaries. They live within boundaries. This is why domain-driven design is helpful and harmful in the same motions.

Schema reuse matters a lot. API design matters a lot, and MCP design patterns for APIs have introduced new concepts like prompts and resources, but Agent Skills I think hold the most potential for bridging what end-users will actually need. Agent Skills have the potential to exist across domains, and transcend boundaries, while speaking to LLMs and human beings. However, if the data is garbage, the skills will only get us so far. This is why schema coherence matters, and API design matters, and the prompts, resources, and skills we craft will help or hurt depending on the quality of access to our data as defined by schema and APIs.

This is one of the reasons that the technology we choose, the commercial nature, and open-source opportunities matter. The intent of these services and tools will define domains and the boundaries in which software serves, but also the interoperability across these domains and boundaries. Standards will further shape the friction that these services and tools introduce across boundaries. The lack of standardization of the technology and business of our schema and the APIs we provide to access the data we make available via schema helped severely limit the potential of the last open data movement, and will shape how successful we are with the current AI programmability efforts around city systems.

I see schema, APIs, and standards as the technical lubricant to reduce friction across domains and boundaries, allowing information to flow. I see licensing, pricing, and the road maps of the technology we employ as the business lubricant that will allow information to flow. I see stories of the processes that matter to average citizens as the lubricant that will allow, and drive, the flow of information. We need all of this. This is why I approach this project from the outside-in, because it is closest to the stories that matter to citizens, but also the intent of the businesses selling technology to the city, and then to the websites and data portals published to enable the flow of information. I'd love to go deeper into the internals of each domain to understand the backend systems, and the people and politics that shape these outputs, but I needed to start from the outside-in.
